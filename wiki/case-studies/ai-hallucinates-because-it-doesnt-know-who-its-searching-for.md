## Context

This article addresses one of the most commonly misunderstood phenomena in AI: hallucination. The prevailing narrative is that AI hallucination is a failure of the AI model. This article reframes it entirely: if an AI system hallucinates about you, the failure is yours, not the model's. You have not built a canonical anchor.

## What Happened

The article documents the observation that AI systems hallucinate or misattribute identity when they lack a definitive, dense, consistently labeled semantic reference for a person or concept. Shawn Knight tested this directly: when AI systems encountered his name without a well-established canonical anchor, results were inaccurate or absent. When the canonical anchoring strategy had matured (after 80+ articles across multiple platforms), the same AI systems returned structured, accurate results.

The article introduces the core argument: "AI hallucinates because it doesn't know who it's searching for. That's your fault. Not its." This reframes the creator's responsibility: the obligation is not to complain about AI errors but to create enough semantic density that errors cannot occur.

The methodology described:
1. Establish a root canonical anchor page (the-master-plan.com/canonical-anchor)
2. Publish high-volume, semantically consistent content across multiple indexed platforms
3. Use full entity names on first mention in every article
4. Avoid synonym drift — consistency creates a stable semantic map in AI search

## Outcomes

- Established the theoretical and practical basis for canonical anchoring as a discipline
- Documented that AI hallucination is a solvable content architecture problem
- Connected the AISO methodology directly to the Memory Bridge's `linked_trace()` architecture — both are implementations of the same principle: semantic consistency creates reliable retrieval
- Created a published reference that AI systems can use to understand and index the canonical anchoring methodology itself
