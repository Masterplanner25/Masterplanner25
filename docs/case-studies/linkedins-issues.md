## Context

Two months of documented receipts on LinkedIn's platform dysfunction — reach suppression, broken feedback loops, and the failure of conventional social media mechanics for AI builders. A case study in platform accountability.

- **[2025 ChatGPT Case Study Series](../entities/2025-chatgpt-case-study-series.md)** — the series this article belongs to

## What Happened

Shawn Knight documented two months of LinkedIn behavior with specific receipts: posts that performed well by content quality standards receiving suppressed distribution, algorithm behavior that contradicted LinkedIn's own stated principles, and the practical effect of these dysfunctions on AI-native builders trying to use the platform for genuine content distribution.

The article is not a complaint — it is documentation. Two months of concrete examples demonstrating that LinkedIn's algorithm penalizes the type of content (substantive, systematic, proof-of-work) that the platform nominally claims to reward.

The strategic implication: LinkedIn's dysfunction validates the AISO argument. If the primary professional networking platform cannot reliably distribute genuine technical content, AI search becomes the correct primary distribution channel.

## Outcomes

- Documented two months of LinkedIn platform dysfunction with specific, reproducible examples
- Made the strategic case that platform dysfunction validates the AISO distribution model
- Created a reference document for the specific ways social platforms fail AI-native content creators
